# Citation & Originality — paraphrase vs quote vs plagiarism

For `originality-transformer` and `plagiarism-scanner`. The job is to make source-using drafts genuinely original *and* properly credited — not to hide borrowing.

## The three relationships to a source
1. **Quotation** — source's exact words, in quotes, with attribution. Always shows as a similarity match; that's fine when cited.
2. **Paraphrase** — source's *idea* in your own structure and words, with attribution. Legitimate when genuinely reworded **and** credited.
3. **Plagiarism** — source's words or distinctive structure reused **without** attribution, or a "paraphrase" that only swaps a few words. Not acceptable. Surface-level synonym-swapping of someone else's sentence is still plagiarism even if a checker misses it.

## The "transformed enough?" test
A paraphrase is genuine only if **all** hold:
- **New structure** — sentence and paragraph order are yours, not the source's reskinned.
- **Synthesis** — you combine the idea with others / your own analysis, not echo one source.
- **Your analysis** — there's a point, judgment, or framing the source didn't have.
- **Could you write it without looking?** If you needed the source open to keep the phrasing, it isn't transformed.

If it fails the test → either quote+cite it, or genuinely rewrite from understanding, or cut it.

## Attribution formats
- **EN inline:** "According to Smith (2024), …" / "Smith argues that … (2024)."
- **RO inline:** "Potrivit lui Smith (2024), …" / "Smith susține că … (2024)."
- **Link/source list** for web sources; keep a running citation list in the Transformed Draft.
- A direct quote **with** citation is honest even though it shows as a match — never strip a citation to lower a similarity score.

## Copyright basics (not legal advice)
- Copyright protects **expression**, not **ideas/facts**. You can reuse a fact; you can't reuse the wording or distinctive structure.
- **Public domain** and properly **licensed** (e.g. CC, with terms followed) material can be reused per its terms — still attribute where required.
- Translation of someone else's text is a derivative work — translating to dodge a checker is still copying.

## Note for Fri's children's-book work
- **Retelling** a public-domain tale (Brothers Grimm, Aesop, folklore) is fine — but write it fresh in your own words and voice; don't copy a specific modern author's retelling.
- A **specific published version** (a living author's text/illustrations) is protected — be inspired by the tradition, not by their expression.
- Original characters, plots, and phrasing are the safe, valuable default. When in doubt, transform fully and create rather than adapt.
