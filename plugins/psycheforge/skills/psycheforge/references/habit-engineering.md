# Habit Engineering

Behavioral-science foundation for building or breaking habits. Grades: `[EVIDENCE-BASED]` / `[MODERATE-EVIDENCE]` / `[TRADITIONAL-SYMBOLIC]` / `[EXPERIMENTAL]` / `[NOT-SUPPORTED]`. See evidence-map.md for the parallel grading of adjacent techniques (implementation intentions, hypnosis, affirmations).

## 1. Fogg Behavior Model (B=MAP)

BJ Fogg's core equation: **Behavior happens when Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt converge at the same moment.** If a behavior doesn't happen, one of the three is missing — diagnose which one before blaming "discipline." High-motivation/low-ability tasks need simplification; high-ability/low-motivation tasks need a better prompt or a smaller ask.

**Tiny Habits method** (Fogg, 2019): the operational recipe is "After I [ANCHOR MOMENT], I will [TINY BEHAVIOR]. Then I celebrate." Three parts:
- **Anchor**: an existing, reliable routine ("after I pour my coffee") — this is the Prompt in B=MAP.
- **Tiny behavior**: scaled down to <30 seconds, near-zero motivation required (2 pushups, not "start exercising").
- **Celebration ("Shine")**: an immediate self-generated positive emotion (a fist pump, "Victory!") meant to wire in the association before the behavior scales up.

Caveat: celebration is Fogg's central theoretical claim, but no peer-reviewed study has isolated whether self-celebration accelerates habit formation versus a no-celebration control. Treat it as `[EXPERIMENTAL]` — plausible, unfalsified in practice, not independently verified.

## 2. Cue → Routine → Reward → Craving (Duhigg)

Charles Duhigg's habit loop: **cue** (internal — a feeling; or external — time, place, smell) triggers a **routine** (the behavior), which delivers a **reward** the brain registers as worth repeating. His later refinement adds **craving**: the anticipation of the reward is what actually pulls a person through the loop — not the reward itself. Cravings, once established, run largely outside awareness; users often can't name what they're craving even as it drives the behavior.

**The golden rule of habit change**: keep the cue and the reward, change only the routine. This is the load-bearing principle for habit *replacement* (see §5).

## 3. Atomic Habits: the Four Laws (Clear)

James Clear operationalizes the same cue→craving→response→reward loop into four actionable laws, each invertible to break a habit:

| Law | To build | To break |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Cue | Make it obvious | Make it invisible |
| 2. Craving | Make it attractive | Make it unattractive |
| 3. Response | Make it easy | Make it difficult |
| 4. Reward | Make it satisfying | Make it unsatisfying |

Practical levers under each: environment redesign (Law 1), temptation bundling (Law 2, see §6), reducing steps/friction (Law 3, see §4), and visible tracking or immediate reward stacking (Law 4).

**Identity-based framing**: Clear's parallel claim is that lasting habit change works backward from identity, not forward from outcome — "every action is a vote for the type of person you wish to become." No single instance changes self-belief, but repeated votes accumulate into identity evidence. Practical use: frame the habit as "I am becoming someone who ___" rather than "I want to ___" — this ties directly into identity-statement rules in narrative-methods.md (which also carries the Wood et al. 2009 caveat about statements landing outside believable range).

## 4. Environment, Friction, and Context (Wendy Wood)

Wendy Wood's research (USC, 30+ years) reframes habits as **context-cued responses**, not products of willpower or motivation strength — an estimated 43% of daily actions run on habit rather than active decision-making. Nearly half of behavior occurs in the same location daily and becomes cued by that environment (place, time, preceding action, people present).

- **Willpower has limits environment doesn't**: you cannot reliably out-discipline a strong environmental cue; you can redesign the environment.
- **Friction is the operational lever**: ease of repetition depends on friction (time, distance, effort required). Add friction to unwanted behaviors (move the snack to a high shelf, log out of the app); remove friction from wanted ones (lay out gym clothes the night before).
- **Context disruption breaks old habits for free**: when context changes wholesale (moving house, new job), old habitual cues stop firing automatically — this is a genuine opportunity window, not just an inconvenience.

**Friction audit** (practical protocol): list the 3 steps immediately before the unwanted behavior and the 3 steps immediately before the wanted alternative. Add one friction point to the unwanted chain; remove one from the wanted chain. Re-test for a week.

**Habit stacking**: chain a new habit onto an existing, already-automatic one ("after [existing habit], I will [new habit]") — functionally the same mechanism as Fogg's anchor-moment prompt (§1) and Gollwitzer's implementation intentions (§7), applied to a pre-existing cue rather than a novel one.

## 5. Breaking Habits: Three Levers

1. **Cue elimination** — remove or avoid the trigger entirely (don't keep the substance in the house; unfollow the accounts). Most effective when the cue is a discrete object/location, weakest when the cue is an internal state (stress, boredom) that can't be "removed."
2. **Friction addition** — make the routine harder to execute (see §4). Buys time for deliberate override between cue and routine.
3. **Substitution (Duhigg's golden rule)** — keep the cue and the reward identical; swap only the routine. E.g., stress (cue) → reward is relief/distraction — keep both, replace "smoke a cigarette" with "walk around the block." This preserves the craving-satisfaction loop instead of fighting it head-on, which is why it outperforms pure suppression in practice.

**Urge surfing** (Marlatt, early 1980s, Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention): treat a craving as a wave with a rise, peak, and natural subsidence rather than something to fight or obey. Observe the bodily sensations, thoughts, and emotions of the urge without judgment or action; most urges peak and pass within roughly 30 minutes when not fed by avoidance or action. Useful as a bridge technique during the friction window created by levers 1–2 above.

## 6. Context and Timing Tools

**Fresh-start effect** (Dai, Milkman & Riis, 2014, *Management Science*): temporal landmarks — a new week, month, year, birthday, holiday — measurably increase goal-initiation behavior (diet-related searches, gym visits, goal commitments spike right after these dates). Mechanism: landmarks create a perceived discontinuity that mentally files past "imperfect self" into a closed prior period, prompting a big-picture view and aspirational action. Practical use: anchor a new protocol's start date to a genuine landmark rather than an arbitrary Tuesday — it's a real, replicated nudge, not superstition.

**Temptation bundling** (Milkman, Minson & Volpp, 2014, *Management Science*): pair a "should" behavior with a "want" — restrict an enjoyable stimulus (an addictive audiobook, a favorite show) to only be available during the target behavior (only at the gym). Field experiment: participants given gym-only-access iPods loaded with captivating audiobooks visited the gym 51% more often than controls over 10 weeks. Caveat: the effect was not perfectly durable — visits declined toward the end of the study, notably after a holiday interruption (Thanksgiving) broke the routine, underscoring that temptation bundling helps initiation more reliably than it guarantees long-term maintenance on its own.

## 7. Implementation Intentions ("If-Then" Plans)

Gollwitzer & Sheeran's 2006 meta-analysis (94 independent tests) found implementation intentions — pre-committing "if situation X occurs, I will do Y" — produced a medium-to-large effect on goal attainment, **d = 0.65** (95% CI 0.6–0.7). This is among the best-replicated self-regulation tools in psychology (see evidence-map.md §3 for the fuller citation trail, including a 2024 642-test meta-analysis replicating the effect range .27–.66). Mechanism: pre-loading the response into a specific situational cue removes the need for in-the-moment willpower or decision-making — the plan itself becomes the prompt.

Applied to habits specifically: "If [cue/trigger], then I will [routine]" is functionally identical to Fogg's anchor-moment recipe (§1) and Wood's habit-stacking (§4) — three independent research traditions converging on the same structural fix.

## 8. Automaticity Timeline

Lally et al. (2010, *European Journal of Social Psychology*): 96 participants tracked a self-chosen daily habit for up to 84 days, self-rating automaticity daily. **Median time to reach 95% of asymptotic automaticity was 66 days, with a range of 18–254 days** depending on the person and behavior. The popular "21 days" claim has no basis in this data. Missing a single day did not measurably disrupt the automaticity curve — occasional lapses are tolerated by the underlying process, which is itself useful reassurance for relapse planning (§9).

Practical implication: set protocol minimums in the 8–10 week range, not 3 weeks, and warn users the range is wide — some habits genuinely take 4x longer than others to automate.

## 9. Relapse Planning: Lapse ≠ Relapse

Marlatt & Gordon's relapse-prevention framework distinguishes a **lapse** (a single instance of the old behavior) from a **relapse** (abandonment of the change goal and return to the old pattern) — research across addictive behaviors shows a lapse increases relapse risk but the progression is not inevitable.

**Abstinence Violation Effect (AVE)**: the guilt/shame/failure response after a lapse is what drives the lapse→relapse progression, not the lapse itself. How a person *attributes* the lapse (a permanent personal failure vs. a single-instance, situational slip) predicts whether it escalates. This is the single most actionable insight for relapse planning — the plan should target the *attribution*, not just the behavior.

**If-then recovery plans**: pre-write the recovery response before a lapse happens, using the same implementation-intention structure as §7 — "If I [lapse], then I will [specific next action: e.g., no diet-starts-over narrative, just resume the plan at the next scheduled point], and I will not treat this as evidence the plan failed."

**Streak psychology, honestly**: "don't break the chain" tracking (Seinfeld method) works because visible streaks trigger real dopamine anticipation and loss-aversion, which is a genuine motivational lever — not a gimmick. But the mechanism is also the failure mode: streak systems create an all-or-nothing frame where one missed day feels like total failure, and the abstinence-violation dynamic above kicks in at exactly the wrong moment. Streaks are most durable when the habit is scaled small enough (§1, tiny habits) to survive normal life friction, and should be paired with an explicit if-then recovery plan (this section) so a broken streak has a scripted next step instead of triggering abandonment.
