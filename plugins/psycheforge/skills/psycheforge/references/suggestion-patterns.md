# Suggestion Patterns — Self-Hypnosis Reference

For `the-suggestionist`. Self-hypnosis = self-directed focused attention combined with suggestion, used to shift perception, sensation, or behavior toward a chosen outcome. It is not sleep, not unconsciousness, and not mind control — the user stays capable of rejecting any suggestion.

## 1. Framing & Evidence Grade

`[MODERATE-EVIDENCE]`. Hypnosis has one of the more researched track records in the self-regulation space, but strength varies sharply by target:

- **Medical/procedural distress + acute pain**: strongest evidence. A 20-year meta-analytic review found the most robust evidence for hypnosis in patients undergoing invasive medical procedures (12 reviews, 79 studies) and in pain patients (4 reviews, 65 studies) (Rosendahl et al. 2024, *Frontiers in Psychology*).
- **Anxiety**: positive but more mixed. Valentine et al. (2019) found hypnosis effective for reducing anxiety; multiple applications (stress reduction, anxiety, well-being) were rated "highly effective" by ≥70% of surveyed clinicians in the same 2024 review.
- **Chronic pain specifically**: genuinely contested. One meta-analysis of 85 controlled trials found a moderate decrease in pain intensity and interference; a separate recent analysis found no significant effect on chronic pain. Duration matters — effects were moderate-to-large at ≥8 sessions, small and non-significant under 8 sessions (Langlois, Perrochon et al. 2022, musculoskeletal/neuropathic pain meta-analysis).
- **Hypnotic suggestibility is a real moderator**: high-suggestible individuals get ~42% clinically meaningful pain reduction with direct analgesic suggestion vs. ~29% for medium-suggestible — meaning self-hypnosis works better for some people than others, honestly.

Bottom line for the studio: frame self-hypnosis as a plausible-but-variable adjunct, never a guaranteed fix, and never a replacement for medical treatment of pain or diagnosed anxiety disorders.

**Expectation is a named mechanism, not a trick.** Response expectancy (believing an outcome will occur, which itself changes physiology/behavior) is a documented, studied component of hypnotic response — not deception. Naming this to the user ("part of why this works is that you expect it to, and that expectation is a real lever") is honest, not undermining.

## 2. Structure of a Self-Hypnosis Session

Six phases. Typical total: 10–20 minutes for a focused session; can compress to 5 minutes with practice.

1. **Preparation** (1–2 min) — safe seated/reclined position, permission to stop anytime, state the intention/suggestion in one sentence before starting.
2. **Induction** (2–4 min) — narrows attention, begins the shift. Pick one:
   - *Eye fixation*: fix gaze on a point until eyes tire and want to close.
   - *Progressive relaxation*: scan and release tension head-to-toe (see somatic-protocols.md for the PMR method it borrows from).
   - *Breath count*: count breaths down from 10, one number per exhale.
3. **Deepening** (1–3 min) — intensifies the state. Pick one:
   - *Staircase*: imagine walking down a staircase, one step per count, going "deeper" with each step.
   - *Countdown*: count 10→1 with a suggestion of increasing relaxation per number.
4. **Suggestion delivery** (2–5 min) — the payload; see writing rules in §4 and language patterns in §3.
5. **Post-hypnotic bridge** (30–60 sec) — links the suggestion to a real-world cue ("when you [specific trigger], you will notice [specific response]") so the state transfers to daily life.
6. **Emergence** (1 min) — count up 1→5 (or reverse the induction count), suggest alertness returning with each number, open eyes, orient to the room, stretch.

Timing note: shorter/lighter for state-shift use (pre-performance calm), longer for identity-level suggestion work. Never rush emergence — allow the full count even if the user feels alert early.

## 3. Ericksonian / Indirect Language Patterns

Origin: developed by psychiatrist **Milton H. Erickson**, then catalogued and named as a pattern set by Richard Bandler and John Grinder in *Patterns of the Hypnotic Techniques of Milton H. Erickson, M.D.* (Vol. 1, 1975; Vol. 2 with Judith DeLozier, on nonverbal patterning). This book is the actual origin of the catalogue below — it is a real, verifiable text, distinct from later NLP marketing claims (see §6).

- **Truisms & yes-sets** — string obviously-true statements to build agreement momentum before the suggestion. *"You're sitting here, you're breathing, and you're ready to notice something new."*
- **Presuppositions** — embed the desired outcome as already-assumed, not offered as a choice. *"As you begin to feel calmer, you might notice which muscle relaxes first."*
- **Embedded suggestions** — bury a directive inside a larger sentence, often marked by a shift in tone. *"I don't know exactly when you'll **feel confident and ready**, but your mind does."*
- **Binds of comparable alternatives** (illusion of choice, both options lead to the goal) — *"You can close your eyes now, or in a few more breaths — whichever feels right."*
- **Utilization** — incorporate whatever is already happening (a noise, a sensation, resistance) into the suggestion rather than fighting it. *"That sound you notice can help you drop even deeper."*
- **Metaphor / nested story** — deliver the suggestion indirectly through an analogous story, letting the unconscious make the mapping. *"I once knew someone who found that the harder they gripped a rope, the more tired their hand became — until they simply let go."*
- **Future pacing** — mentally rehearse the suggestion already working in a specific future moment. *"Notice yourself next week, in that meeting, feeling that same steadiness."*

## 4. Writing Rules for Suggestions

- **Present-tense / process phrasing** — describe the change as happening now, as a process, not a future promise. "You are becoming more at ease" beats "You will be calm."
- **Positively framed (toward, not away)** — state what to move toward, not what to avoid; the mind struggles to "not" a suggestion. "Calm and steady" beats "not anxious."
- **Sensory-concrete** — anchor in a specific sense (a feeling in the hands, a color, a temperature), not abstractions. Concrete imagery is what the induction and deepening phases are built to prime.
- **Ego-syntonic** — align with the user's own stated values and goals; never suggest something the user would reject in waking state. Self-hypnosis has no external hypnotist to override the user's judgment — alignment is what makes it stick.
- **Rhythmic repetition** — restate the core suggestion 2–3 times with slight variation; rhythm itself supports the relaxed-focus state.
- **Avoid authoritarian commands for self-use** — "You must," "You will absolutely" reads as coercive even to oneself and tends to trigger resistance. Indirect/permissive phrasing (§3) works better precisely because there's no external authority relationship to lean on.

## 5. SAFETY (mandatory)

- **NO age regression or memory recovery work.** Hypnotic age regression is subject to the same distortion as ordinary memory, and there is no reason to expect it recovers real childhood memories more reliably than waking recall — it reliably risks creating false ones. Elizabeth Loftus's research (including the "lost in the mall" paradigm — ~25% false-memory acceptance in the original 1995 study, ~35% in a large 2023 replication) established that age regression, guided visualization, and hypnosis are specifically flagged techniques for creating false or pseudo-memories. This studio's hypnotic work is **forward-facing self-suggestion only** — never regression, never "recovering" anything.
- **No suggestions that override or mask pain that could signal a medical issue.** Pain is information. Suggestion for pain *coping* (comfort, distraction, reinterpretation) is different from suggesting pain *away* without knowing its cause — the latter risks concealing something that needs a doctor.
- **Abreaction protocol** — if strong, unexpected distress/emotion surfaces during a session: stop the induction, open eyes immediately, name 3 things visible in the room (grounding), state date/location aloud, end the session. Do not push through distress to "finish" the suggestion.
- **Contraindications**: history of psychosis or active psychotic symptoms (altered-attention states can interact unpredictably with psychotic process; professional hypnotherapy bodies list active psychosis as a contraindication) and dissociative disorders (hypnosis is itself a form of focused dissociation — in someone with a dissociative disorder it can worsen symptoms without specialist handling) → **professional-only, not self-hypnosis.**
- **Never during or before driving, or operating machinery.** The relaxed, narrowed-attention state is incompatible with the alertness both require.

## 6. NLP Status Note (honesty required)

Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP) — the broader framework Bandler and Grinder built after their Erickson modeling work — is widely classified as pseudoscience by the current research literature. A meta-analysis reviewing 63 NLP studies found over 80% could not be reproduced or hold up under falsifiability testing; critics point to loosely defined constructs (representational systems, submodalities, eye-accessing cues) that aren't tied to established cognitive/neuroscience models, and to unfalsifiable framing ("it works if the practitioner is skilled").

**What this studio borrows and what it doesn't**: the language-pattern catalogue in §3 above (truisms, presuppositions, embedded suggestions, etc.) is drawn from the *documented verbal patterns of a real clinician* (Erickson) as recorded by direct observation — that's craft, testable as rhetoric/communication technique on its own terms. What we explicitly do **not** borrow is NLP's pseudo-neurological apparatus (claims about eye movements revealing internal processing, "neural" reprogramming metaphors, VAK learning-style claims) — those claims are not supported and are not used or implied anywhere in this studio's material.
