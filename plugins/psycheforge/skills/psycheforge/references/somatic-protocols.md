# Somatic Protocols — Breath & Body Reference

For `the-somatist`. Breath and body-based techniques regulate the autonomic nervous system directly and fast — usually within minutes — making them the studio's tool for immediate state-shifts, distinct from the slower cognitive/narrative registers.

## 1. Framing & Evidence Status

`[MODERATE-EVIDENCE]` as a category, with real spread inside it. Slow/paced breathing has one of the better-replicated effect profiles in the self-regulation space: a meta-analysis found significant small-to-medium effects of breathing practices (mainly slow-paced) on subjective stress, anxiety, and depressive symptoms vs. non-breathwork controls — though most included studies carried moderate risk of bias, so "real but not overstated" is the honest read. Some individual protocols below (physiological sigh, PMR) have their own strong single-study or meta-analytic backing; others (box breathing, 4-7-8) are traditional/popularized practices with much thinner direct evidence, which is labeled honestly per protocol. Body-based techniques act on measurable physiology (heart-rate variability, respiratory rate, muscle tension) — this is a mechanistic, not mystical, category.

## 2. Protocols

### Physiological Sigh / Cyclic Sighing
- **Steps**: two consecutive inhales through the nose (short first inhale, then a second smaller "top-up" inhale) followed by one long, slow exhale through the mouth.
- **Dose**: 5 minutes/day (studied dose); can be used as a single 1–3 breath spot-fix for acute spikes.
- **When to use**: fastest-acting option for an acute anxiety/stress spike; also as a daily practice.
- **Evidence note**: Balban, Yilmaz Balban, Huberman, Spiegel et al., Stanford, *Cell Reports Medicine* (2023) — 111 healthy adults, 28 days, four techniques compared (cyclic sighing, box breathing, cyclic hyperventilation, mindfulness). Cyclic sighing produced the largest daily gains in positive mood and the biggest drop in resting respiratory rate of the four — effects measurable after a single 5-minute session and increasing with daily practice. Mechanism: the double inhale reinflates collapsed alveoli; the long exhale drives parasympathetic (vagal) activation.

### Coherent / Resonance / Paced Breathing
- **Steps**: breathe at ~5:5 second inhale:exhale ratio (roughly 5.5–6 breaths/minute total).
- **Dose**: sessions of 5–20 minutes; a single 5-minute session can shift state mood.
- **When to use**: daily HRV-building practice, or pre-sleep/pre-performance settling.
- **Evidence note**: this is the best-evidenced rate range in the whole file. Slow breathing at 4.5–6.5 breaths/min optimally balances sympathetic/parasympathetic tone for most adults. Meta-analytic data: paced breathing shows markedly higher HRV time-domain measures than spontaneous breathing (SDNN g=1.64, RMSSD g=0.93, both p<.01) and is associated with lower subjective stress/anxiety scores. Direction of effect (more HRV, less subjective distress) is consistently supported across systematic reviews, though absolute effect sizes vary by study.

### Box Breathing (4-4-4-4)
- **Steps**: inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4; repeat.
- **Dose**: 2–5 minutes.
- **When to use**: pre-performance, tactical/quick reset (popularized in military and first-responder training).
- **Evidence note**: included as one of the four arms in the Stanford 2023 cyclic-sighing study — it improved mood and lowered respiratory rate too, but *less* than cyclic sighing on both measures in that head-to-head. Treat as a reasonable, easy-to-remember option, not the top-tier pick.

### 4-7-8 Breathing
- **Steps**: inhale 4 counts through the nose, hold 7, exhale 8 through the mouth.
- **Dose**: 4 breath cycles, 1–2x/day.
- **When to use**: popularized for sleep onset and acute calming.
- **Evidence note**: traditional pranayama-derived technique popularized by Dr. Andrew Weil; label honestly as **popularizer-branded, thin direct trial evidence** compared to the physiological-sigh and coherent-breathing protocols above. The general "long exhale relative to inhale" mechanism it uses is supported (see next entry); the specific 4-7-8 ratio and breath-hold has not been isolated in controlled trials the way cyclic sighing has.

### Extended-Exhale Ratio Principle
- The common mechanism behind several protocols above: **exhale longer than inhale** to bias the autonomic nervous system toward parasympathetic (calming) rather than sympathetic (arousing) tone. Documented as a driver of the cyclic-sighing effect and consistent with the broader slow-breathing literature. Use this principle to explain *why* several different-looking protocols work similarly — it is the shared active ingredient.

### Progressive Muscle Relaxation (Jacobson)
- **Steps**: tense a muscle group hard for ~5 seconds, then release and notice the contrast for ~15–20 seconds; move systematically through muscle groups (feet → legs → abdomen → hands → arms → shoulders → face).
- **Dose**: full-body pass takes 10–20 minutes; can be shortened to major groups only (~5 min).
- **When to use**: general tension release, pre-sleep, anxiety management, whenever body awareness is low.
- **Evidence note**: one of the better-supported techniques in this file. Systematic reviews report consistently high anxiety/stress reduction and sleep-quality improvement; a broader meta-analysis of relaxation training for anxiety disorders found a medium-to-large effect size (Cohen's d = 0.51–0.57). Developed by physician Edmund Jacobson in 1938 — genuinely old, genuinely still supported.

### Body Scan
- **Steps**: sequentially bring attention to each body region (no tensing, just noticing sensation) from feet to head, without judgment or effort to change anything.
- **Dose**: 5–20 minutes.
- **When to use**: as a bridge into self-hypnosis induction (see suggestion-patterns.md §2), or as a standalone mindfulness/interoception practice.
- **Evidence note**: shares its evidence base with mindfulness-based interventions broadly; effective for relaxation and body awareness, generally gentler and slower-acting than PMR.

### Grounding 5-4-3-2-1
- **Steps**: name 5 things you can see, 4 things you can touch, 3 things you can hear, 2 things you can smell, 1 thing you can taste.
- **Dose**: 1–3 minutes.
- **When to use**: acute anxiety, dissociation, panic, flashback — anchors attention back into the present sensory environment.
- **Evidence note**: this exact 5-4-3-2-1 protocol hasn't been isolated in large controlled trials, but it draws on well-supported CBT/DBT grounding principles, and a 2025 trial in nursing students with test anxiety found significant anxiety-score reductions after the intervention. Broader grounding-strategy research (Journal of Traumatic Stress) supports grounding for reducing anxiety and dissociative symptoms in PTSD. Honest label: **plausible mechanism (present-moment sensory anchoring), thin isolated-technique evidence, borrows credibility from the broader grounding/CBT literature** — not a substitute for treatment of clinical anxiety, panic disorder, or PTSD.

### Cold Exposure (brief note)
- Brief cold exposure (cold water on face/hands, cold shower) triggers the mammalian dive reflex, producing rapid parasympathetic activation and heart-rate deceleration — a real, mechanistic effect. Kept deliberately brief here: evidence is real for the acute physiological response, thinner for claimed downstream benefits (mood, resilience) at the population level, and it carries its own cardiovascular caution (see Safety). Not a breath protocol — included because it's often paired with breathwork (see Wim Hof note in Safety) and the pairing is exactly where the risk concentrates.

### Posture / Embodiment
- Honest note required: the "power pose" literature (Carney, Cuddy & Yap 2010 — claiming expansive posture shifts testosterone/cortisol and felt power) is one of psychology's clearest replication failures. Lead author Dana Carney issued a full statement in 2016 stating "I do not believe that 'power pose' effects are real" and describing how the original data were handled to produce the apparent effect; she has since discouraged further study of the topic. The subjective "I feel more confident when I stand differently" effect may still hold for some individuals (self-report, non-hormonal), but **any claim about posture changing hormones or measurable power should not be made** — that specific claim did not replicate and its own author disavowed it.

## 3. State-Shifting Quick Drills

- **Pre-performance anxiety**: 3–5 physiological sighs (30–90 sec) immediately before, then one round of box breathing while waiting to start.
- **Rumination interrupt**: 5-4-3-2-1 grounding (1–3 min) to break the internal loop by forcing external sensory attention, followed by 2 minutes of coherent breathing to hold the shift.
- **Anger cool-down**: extended-exhale breathing (any protocol above using a longer exhale than inhale) for 2–3 minutes — the mechanism directly counters the sympathetic activation of anger; avoid box breathing's holds if breath already feels constricted.
- **Sleep onset**: PMR full-body pass (10–15 min) or 4-7-8 for 4 cycles, done lying down, lights off; avoid physiological sigh or anything with a hyperventilation component right before sleep (too activating for some).

## 4. SAFETY (mandatory)

- **Hyperventilation-style practices (Wim Hof-style rapid breathing + breath-holds) carry real, documented risk**: the combination of CO2 depletion (hypocapnia) and breath-holding can cause loss of consciousness (syncope), particularly in anyone prone to vasovagal fainting. This is amplified when paired with cold exposure.
- **NEVER in or near water** — documented deaths have occurred from loss of consciousness during hyperventilation-style breathing combined with water immersion (bath, pool, open water); this is rule one in the Wim Hof organization's own published safety guidance, and the Divers Alert Network names voluntary hyperventilation before breath-holding as the primary avoidable cause of shallow-water blackout.
- **Never while driving or operating machinery** — any technique that alters alertness or can cause dizziness/lightheadedness (all rapid/deep-breathing protocols; less relevant to slow paced breathing but still caution) is incompatible with tasks requiring full alertness.
- **Pregnancy**: avoid high-ventilation/rapid breathing protocols (cyclic hyperventilation, Wim Hof-style), especially first trimester — they can shift blood pressure and increase dizziness/fainting risk, which carries risk for the pregnancy. Slow/coherent breathing and PMR are generally considered gentler alternatives but check with a care provider.
- **Cardiovascular conditions**: uncontrolled hypertension, recent cardiac events, aneurysm, or arrhythmia → avoid any protocol causing sudden heart-rate/blood-pressure shifts (rapid breathing, breath-holds, cold exposure); these are the conditions most directly implicated in breathwork risk case reports.
- **Epilepsy / seizure history**: avoid rapid, deep breathing protocols — faster/deeper breathing carries a documented, if small, elevated seizure-risk association in this population.
- **Panic disorder**: not an automatic disqualifier — many people with panic disorder benefit from slow breathing — but rapid-breathing protocols specifically can produce sensations (breathlessness, chest tightness, dizziness) that mimic and can trigger a panic attack (interoceptive triggers). Start with slow/coherent breathing only; introduce anything faster-paced cautiously if at all.
- **Stop rules**: if dizziness, tingling/numbness beyond mild, chest pain, visual disturbance, or any sense of "about to faint" occurs — stop immediately, return to normal breathing, sit or lie down. Any protocol producing real distress (not just unfamiliarity) should be discontinued, not pushed through.
